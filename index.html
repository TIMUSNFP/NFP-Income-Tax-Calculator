A note on the math

The myth of the right regime.

Every Budget season, the same question floods every financial advisor's inbox: New regime or Old? Most salaried employees pick based on habit — or their employer's default TDS deduction. Depending on deductions and income level, taxpayers who run the math can save significantly by choosing the optimal regime — often the difference is the entire Section 80C + home loan benefit.

The zero-tax promise — and the fine print.

Budget 2025 announced zero tax on income up to ₹12 lakh. What the Finance Act actually says: Section 87A provides a rebate up to ₹60,000 for taxable income ≤ ₹12L. For salaried employees, the standard deduction of ₹75,000 pushes this to ₹12.75L gross. The slabs below are what the Income Tax portal uses — not an estimate.

Common mistakes

Why most people overpay.

Four things the math exposes every time — that the average taxpayer never checks before filing.

01
For most, new regime wins — unless deductions are large
Old regime needs ₹3.75L–₹5.5L in deductions to beat new regime on ₹12–20L income. 80C alone (₹1.5L) doesn't move the needle. Taxpayers with home loan interest + HRA + NPS may find old regime substantially better.
02
The ₹12.75L cliff for salaried employees
Gross ₹12.75L → taxable ₹12L after SD → ₹0 tax. Gross ₹13L → taxable ₹12.25L → marginal relief → you pay exactly ₹25,000 (not ₹63,750). One of the most-missed clauses in the Finance Act.
03
Employer NPS works in both regimes
80CCD(2) — employer NPS contribution — is deductible in both New and Old regime. Up to 14% of Basic+DA (aggregate cap ₹7.5L). If your employer offers it, claim it regardless of which regime you pick.
04
Surcharge caps matter above ₹50L
Old regime surcharge: up to 37% on income above ₹5 crore. New regime caps at 25%. For high earners, new regime saves on both the slab rate AND the surcharge — a compounding advantage.
